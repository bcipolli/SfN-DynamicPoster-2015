The PING study (Pediatric Imaging, Neuropsychology, and Genetics) is a mult-site cross-sectional study of over 1400 subjects ages 3-20. All MRI data were collected with methods for cross-site standardization.

PING portal: front-page PING portal: data explorers

We have access to a data spreadsheet, focusing on developing open-source tools for data download, analysis, and visualization. We focus on cortical surfaces area and thickness (reconstructed for us).

Data are available as a spreadsheet. We develop open-source tools.

Our main measure is asymmetry index: (LH - RH) / (LH + RH)

Regression on asymmetry index over age doesn't show any changes over development. Results are similar when grouping by Gender (male/female) or handedness (left/right/mixed) or both.

There is no correlation between surface area and thickness.

We looked at how asymmetry index (AI) in one area correlated to AI in another area, after removing the effect explained by AI of every other area (methods). This gave us a similarity matrix. Areas are organized according to location; red means a positive correlation (one asymmetry predicts the other); blue means anti-correlation.

Scroll to see: surface area, thickness, and an interactive brain map of surface area.

For surface area, note that in general, if one area is asymmetric, a near by is asymmetric in the opposite direction. This indicates local compensation. Also note that most (partial) correlations are along the diagonal.

Select one area in the left brain (showing asymmetry index (AI)), and see a map of the partial correlations of all other areas with that area's AI.

For thickness, note the positive correlations along the diagonal: if one area is asymmetric, nearby areas are asymmetric in the same direction. Again, note that most (partial) correlations are along the diagonal.

PCA looks at the covariance structure of asymmetries. Click on different "components" to explore.

We are underpowered for a GWAS (genome-wide association study), so we tried only a few (all for surface area): frontal pole (it it stands out in uni- and multivariate analyses), transverse temporal (language area), and PCs 0 and 1.

We found a hit for frontal pole / PC1 (essentially the same): Serine Threonine Kinase 31 (STK31). What it does is a mystery, but it shows interesting expression patterns in development.

  • NIH - "shows testis-specific expression"
  • Allen Institute: mouse - frontal lobe expression in development.
  • Allen Institute: Chimpanzee - active in frontal areas at "0 years"
  • Allen Institute: developing human brain - active in restricted range: 16 pcw - 10 months.
  • Allen Institute human brain - shows widespread expression
